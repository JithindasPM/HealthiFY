The basal metabolic rate (BMR) is the energy your body needs while resting, similar to how much fuel a parked car uses. It powers vital organs like the heart, brain, and lungs. Typically, about 70% of your daily energy goes to maintaining these functions, around 20% goes to physical activity, and about 10% is used for digesting food.

BMR is measured when you are fully rested and your nervous system is calm. It typically makes up the largest part of your daily calorie needs. To find your total daily calories, multiply your BMR by a factor between 1.2 and 1.9, depending on how active you are.

BMR is usually estimated using equations based on statistical data. The Mifflin-St Jeor Equation is the most accurate for most people, but the Katch-McArdle Formula can be better for lean individuals who know their body fat percentage. You can select the equation that fits your needs.

The three equations used by the calculator are listed below

Mifflin - St Jeor Equation :

For men : BMR = 10 W + 6.25 H - 5 A + 5

For women : BMR = 10 W + 6.25 H - 5 A - 161

Revised Harris - Benedict Equation :

For men : BMR = 13.397 W + 4.799 H - 5.677 A + 88.362

For women : BMR = 9.247 W + 3.098 H - 4.330 A + 447.593

Katch - McArdle Formula :

BMR = 370 + 21.6 (1 - F) W

where:

W is body weight in kg

H is body height in cm

A is age

F is body fat in percentage

BMR Variables

Muscle Mass

Aerobic exercises, such as running or cycling, have no effect on BMR. However, anaerobic exercises, such as weight-lifting, indirectly lead to a higher BMR because they build muscle mass, increasing resting energy consumption. The more muscle mass in the physical composition of an individual, the higher the BMR required to sustain their body at a certain level.

Age

As people age, their body composition tends to change, with muscle mass generally decreasing and fat mass increasing. This shift can affect BMI readings, as older adults might have the same BMI as younger individuals but higher body fat. Additionally, BMI cutoffs for children and teenagers are adjusted for age and sex due to varying growth patterns.

Weather

Cold environments raise BMR because of the energy required to create a homeostatic body temperature. Likewise, too much external heat can raise BMR as the body expends energy to cool off internal organs. BMR increases approximately 7% for every increase of 1.36 degrees Fahrenheit in the body's internal temperature.

Genetics

Genetic factors can influence body weight, fat distribution, metabolism, and how the body stores fat. People may inherit tendencies that affect appetite, food intake, and energy expenditure, which can impact their BMI. While lifestyle choices like diet and exercise are key, genetics can predispose certain individuals to higher or lower BMI, making it harder or easier to gain or lose weight..

Diet

Consuming a diet high in calories, unhealthy fats, sugars, and processed foods can lead to weight gain and a higher BMI, while a balanced diet rich in fruits, vegetables, lean proteins, and whole grains helps maintain or lower BMI. The quality and quantity of food intake directly affect body weight, which in turn influences BMI levels.

Pregnancy

Pregnancy increases BMI due to natural weight gain from the baby and related factors. Pre-pregnancy BMI guides healthy weight gain, but BMI during pregnancy isn't a key health measure, as weight gain is expected. Regular monitoring ensures both maternal and fetal health, supporting proper development. Post-pregnancy, BMI can help assess recovery and long-term health.

Supplements

Supplements can influence BMI by affecting metabolism and appetite. Protein powders and weight gain supplements may increase BMI by promoting muscle mass. Conversely, appetite suppressants and fat burners can contribute to weight loss, lowering BMI. Their impact varies based on diet, exercise, and individual metabolism

Modern Wisdom

A 2005 meta-analysis study on BMR* showed that when controlling all factors of metabolic rate, there is still a 26% unknown variance between people. Essentially, an average person eating an average diet will likely have expected BMR values, but there are factors that are still not understood that determines BMR precisely.

Therefore, all BMR calculations, even using the most precise methods through specialists, will not be perfectly accurate in their measurements. Not all human bodily functions are well understood just yet, so calculating total daily energy expenditure (TDEE) derived from BMR estimates are just that, estimates. When working towards any sort of health or fitness goal, BMR can aid in laying down the foundations, but from there on, it has little else to offer. A calculated BMR and thus TDEE may result in unsatisfactory results because of their rough estimates, but maintaining a daily journal of exercise, food consumption, etc., can help track the factors that lead to any given results and help determine what works, as well as what needs to be improved upon. Tracking progress in said journal and making adjustments over time as needed is generally the best indication of progress towards reaching personal goals.
